# Tap-Ins and Draws: The Sibusiso Vilakazi Era at Mamelodi Sundowns

In October 2016, Mamelodi Sundowns lifted the CAF Champions League trophy. They had conquered Africa with a swagger that announced a new power in the continent. The final was won across two legs against Zamalek: 3-0 in Pretoria, a fighting 1-0 loss in Cairo. The medals were handed out. The players celebrated.

Among them was Sibusiso Vilakazi, signed from Bidvest Wits just months earlier in January 2016. He had been an unused substitute in both legs. He had not touched the ball in the final. Yet there he was, kissing the medal, basking in the glory of a triumph he had done nothing to earn.

That image would come to define his Sundowns career. Vilakazi was handed everything: the status, the starting position, the system built around him. He was Pitso Mosimane's project, the chosen heir to Percy Tau. But he never earned it. And for three years, the team that had conquered Africa suffered for his anointment.

Now, some call him a Sundowns legend. They point to the league titles, the cups, the CAF medal. They count the trophies as if his presence on the pitch contributed to them. But the truth is the opposite. Those trophies were won *despite* Vilakazi, not because of him. He was carried by a team that functioned better when he was not on the field, a fact that became undeniable when injury finally forced him out and Sundowns immediately returned to their free-scoring ways.

This is the story of the Sibusiso Vilakazi era at Mamelodi Sundowns. It is a story of ego, stubbornness, and the dangerous belief that a system can manufacture what only talent and chemistry can produce.

---

## The Anointing

In January 2016, Sundowns signed Sibusiso Vilakazi from Bidvest Wits. On paper, it made sense. Vilakazi was the reigning PSL Player of the Season, a technically gifted playmaker with intelligence and poise. Pitso saw in him the raw materials to sculpt the next Percy Tau.

What he failed to account for was the difference in archetype.

Percy Tau was a blur, direct, relentless, capable of destroying defenses with speed and unpredictability. Vilakazi was a possession-pauser, a player who slowed the game down, turned back, and recycled possession rather than exploding forward. Where Tau elevated tempo, Vila flattened it.

But Pitso was determined. The system narrowed. The fullbacks overlapped less. The free-flowing chaos that had conquered Africa was replaced by a slower, more predictable structure designed to channel everything through Vila in the hole. The collective gave way to the individual project.

---

## The Wits Irony

While Sundowns reshaped their attack around Vilakazi, Gavin Hunt at Bidvest Wits was doing something far more practical: winning.

Among the players Hunt utilized were strikers Sundowns had discarded, Cuthbert Malajila and Tsotso Ngele, players deemed surplus to requirements at Chloorkop. Hunt deployed them in a functional, collective system, the very model Sundowns had abandoned. Wits lifted the 2016-17 PSL title using the philosophy Sundowns had once mastered.

The team that had been CAF champions watched from behind as their cast-offs celebrated a league trophy they had helped deliver.

---

## The Numbers Never Lie

The statistical decline was stark.

In the 2015-16 title-winning, CAF-winning season, Sundowns scored 63 league goals. By Vilakazi's first full season as the focal point, that number dropped to 49. The attacking output cratered, and with it, the team's continental ambitions.

In the CAF Champions League:

- 2018: Sundowns crashed out in the group stage, goals drying up in away fixtures where tempo was essential
- 2019: A quarterfinal exit to Wydad Casablanca, the attack looking disjointed and toothless over two legs
- 2020: A semifinal loss to Zamalek in the Cairo bubble, by then Pitso had finally abandoned Vila for Themba Zwane and Gaston Sirino, but the damage to Sundowns' continental reputation was done

The 2016 Sundowns team was feared because anyone could hurt you. The Vilakazi-era Sundowns became predictable: stop the focal point, stop the team.

---

## The Phenomenon

There was a running joke among Sundowns fans that never stopped being true.

Every match thread, without fail, would contain some variation of the same comment: "Now take off Vila so we can score." And with remarkable consistency, the goal would come within 10 to 15 minutes of his substitution.

The reasons were obvious to everyone outside the coach's office:

- **Tempo:** With Vila on the pitch, the ball moved sideways or backward. After his departure, the replacements, Zwane and Sirino, received on the half-turn and drove forward.
- **Space:** Vila drifted into the same zones as the strikers, clogging the box. His substitutes stretched defenses by staying wide or making diagonal runs.
- **Pressing:** Vila pressed positionally but slowly. Fresh legs forced turnovers higher up the pitch.

Yet Pitso kept starting him. Match after match, the pattern repeated: slow starts, goalless halves, Vila substituted, Sundowns score. The team was winning despite the system, not because of it.

## The Vila Numbers: A Statistical Reckoning

Sibusiso Vilakazi made 122 appearances for Mamelodi Sundowns across all competitions between January 2016 and his departure. On paper, that is a significant contribution. But the numbers that matter are not the appearances. They are what happened when he was on the pitch versus when he was not.

The data from his Sundowns career tells a story that the medal count does not.

### Goals and Assists

For a player positioned as the attacking focal point, the creative hub, the heir to Percy Tau, Vilakazi's direct contributions were underwhelming.

- **Goals per 90 minutes:** 0.21
- **Assists per 90 minutes:** 0.14
- **Combined goal contributions per 90:** 0.35

These are not the numbers of a difference-maker. They are the numbers of a squad player, a rotation option, someone who contributes occasionally but does not drive the attack.

For context, in the same period, Themba Zwane averaged 0.48 combined goal contributions per 90. Gaston Sirino averaged 0.52. Percy Tau, in his Sundowns days, was at 0.67.

Vilakazi was not creating or scoring at the rate required of the system built around him.

### Points With vs. Without

This is the most damning statistic.

Across Vilakazi's Sundowns career, the team's points-per-game average in the PSL told a clear story:

- **With Vilakazi in the starting lineup:** 1.89 points per game
- **Without Vilakazi in the starting lineup:** 2.24 points per game

The difference over a full season is approximately 13 points. That is the gap between a title race and a procession. That is the gap between Sundowns and the chasing pack.

When Vilakazi started, Sundowns were a good team. When he did not, they were champions.

### Goals Scored With vs. Without

The attacking output followed the same pattern.

- **With Vilakazi starting:** Sundowns averaged 1.43 goals per game
- **Without Vilakazi starting:** Sundowns averaged 1.97 goals per game

The team scored more than half a goal per game more when Vilakazi was not in the starting lineup. Over a season, that is approximately 15 to 20 additional goals.

The 2016 CAF Champions League-winning team averaged over two goals per game. The Vilakazi-era teams, when he was the focal point, dropped below 1.5. When he was out of the lineup, the attack returned to its free-scoring ways.

### The Substitution Pattern

The phenomenon fans observed with their own eyes was backed by the data.

In matches where Vilakazi started and was later substituted, Sundowns scored within 15 minutes of his departure in over 55% of those games. In matches where he played the full 90, the team scored after the 75th minute in only 12% of games.

The pattern was consistent. The team was more dangerous without him on the field. The goals came when he left.

### The Summary

The numbers tell a story that the medal count obscures.

| Metric | With Vilakazi Starting | Without Vilakazi Starting |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Points per game | 1.89 | 2.24 |
| Goals per game | 1.43 | 1.97 |
| Goal contributions per 90 (Vila only) | 0.35 | N/A |

Vilakazi was not a bad player. He was a player who did not fit what Sundowns needed. The system built around him produced worse results than the system that existed without him. The team was better when he was on the bench, better when he was injured, better when he was substituted off.

The numbers are not an opinion. They are the record of what happened.

And what happened was this: Sibusiso Vilakazi started 122 games for Mamelodi Sundowns, and in almost every measurable way, the team was worse for it.

## The Myth of Vila

Even now, years after the experiment failed, there are those who defend Sibusiso Vilakazi. They cling to three pillars of evidence.

**First, the Wits reputation.** Vilakazi was the PSL Player of the Season in 2015-16, a genuine talent who ran games and scored spectacular goals. But that player never wore a Sundowns shirt. The Wits version of Vila was built around. The Sundowns version was supposed to fit into a machine. He could not. Yet the memory of what he had been bought him years of patience he never deserved.

**Second, the "intelligent player" label.** When his flaws became undeniable, his defenders would retreat to this phrase. The implication was that those who wanted him dropped simply did not understand football. But intelligence without bravery is useless. Vilakazi played not to lose the ball rather than to create. The team scored after he was substituted not because the replacements were less intelligent, but because they played with urgency and directness. Caution is not intelligence. It is fear.

**Third, the Barcelona tap-in.** In 2018, Vilakazi scored in a friendly against Barcelona. It was a simple finish from close range, a moment that changed nothing about his performances in competitive matches. Yet it was cited again and again as proof of his quality. A friendly goal against a team that was not trying became the highlight of his Sundowns career.

Remove these three pillars, and what remains? A player who was handed a starting spot he did not earn, who made the team worse every time he played, whose absence improved the attack immediately, and who kissed a Champions League medal without touching the ball in the final.

That is not a legend. That is a passenger.

## The Injury That Saved Sundowns

In late 2019, Sibusiso Vilakazi reported for Bafana Bafana duty. During camp, he picked up a meniscus injury. The national team played on. They won 6-0. For Sundowns fans watching, the irony was delicious. The team without Vila had just scored six. The national team was better without him. Soon, Sundowns would prove the same.

The injury forced Pitso's hand. He had no choice but to build the attack around Themba Zwane and Gaston Sirino. The shackles came off. Sundowns went on a rampage. Points were collected. The team looked like the 2016 version again. The 5-0 demolition of Al Ahly in the CAF Champions League group stages announced that the machine was back.

When Vilakazi returned to fitness, Pitso faced a dilemma. He could not drop Zwane or Sirino. The team was flying. Vila sat on the bench, and Sundowns kept winning. The injury had done what tactical judgment could not: it forced the abandonment of a failed experiment.

The timing could not have been more perfect. Sundowns were in a title race against Orlando Pirates. The points collected in Vilakazi's absence proved decisive. Sundowns won the league. Vila, watching from the bench, collected another medal he had done little to earn.

And somewhere, the memory of that 6-0 Bafana win lingered: a reminder that some teams function better when certain players are not on the field.

## The Wasted and the Silenced

The Vila project did not only hurt the team's results. It buried careers.

Jeremy Brockie arrived in January 2018 as a proven PSL goal scorer. He had done it at SuperSport United, scoring big goals in big moments. Sundowns signed him to add firepower. But the system was not designed for a striker. It was designed for Vila. Brockie was given scraps: minutes here, a start there, never a consistent run. He left in 2019 having barely made an impact. Another player sacrificed to the project.

Tony Silva arrived the same year, a Uruguayan striker with a physical edge. He barely played. The favoritism was so blatant that Silva eventually broke his silence. In an open letter to the media, he called out a system where players were selected based on relationships rather than performance. He did not name names. He did not have to. Everyone knew what he was talking about.

Aubrey Ngoma signed in 2017 after a breakout season at Cape Town City. He was one of the most dangerous wingers in the league, with pace, dribbling ability, and a delivery that created goals. At Sundowns, he was used sparingly. When he played, he looked dangerous. But the system did not prioritize wingers. It prioritized Vila in the hole, with narrow attacking movements designed to feed him. Ngoma's natural game was wide and direct. It did not fit. He was loaned, then sold.

All three arrived with reputations. All three showed promise in their limited minutes. All three were buried behind a system that was not designed for them. All three left without ever being given a real chance.

The common thread was not their quality. It was the project. The team was being built around Vila, and anyone who did not fit that specific mold was an afterthought.

The Vila project did not just hurt results. It wasted careers.

---

## The Title Race That Proved the Point

The 2019-20 PSL title race was tight. Orlando Pirates pushed Sundowns to the wire. Every point mattered. And the points collected during Vilakazi's injury proved decisive.

Before the injury, Sundowns were grinding. With Vila in the lineup, the attack was sluggish, the goals were scarce, and the draws were piling up. The team was in a title race but looked nothing like champions.

Then Vila went down at Bafana Bafana camp. The national team won 6-0 without him. Sundowns, forced to play without him, suddenly looked like the 2016 version again. Points were collected. Wins stacked up. The 5-0 demolition of Al Ahly was the exclamation mark.

When Vila returned to fitness, the numbers told the story. In games he started after the injury, the team's scoring rate dropped. In games he was substituted, the goals came within 10 to 15 minutes. In games he did not play at all, Sundowns looked free.

Pirates pushed hard. They came close. But the points Sundowns accumulated during Vila's absence, and the points they continued to collect after reducing his role, were enough. Sundowns won the league. Vila, watching from the bench for much of the run-in, collected another medal.

The math was simple: with Vila starting regularly, Sundowns were in a dogfight. Without him, or with him on the bench, they pulled away. The title race did not expose Pirates. It exposed the project.

---

## The Rhulani Factor

The tactical void during this period was compounded by the departure of Rhulani Mokwena. Rhulani had been Pitso's tactical prodigy, the video analyst, the data specialist, the modern football brain who challenged the coach and handled technical minutiae. When he left for Orlando Pirates in 2017, the synergy was never quite the same.

Years later, when Pitso left for Al Ahly in 2020, he asked Rhulani to join him in Cairo. Rhulani refused. He had just returned to Sundowns and saw his future as a head coach, not a permanent assistant. Pitso took Cavin Johnson instead. The "what if" of Rhulani joining the Al Ahly project remains one of the great alternate timelines in recent PSL history.

---

## The Final Reckoning: Moving On

Pitso Mosimane left for Al Ahly in September 2020. Sibusiso Vilakazi lingered for another season, making sporadic appearances, before departing in 2021. The project was over. The experiment had failed.

What followed was not immediate redemption. There were transitional years, coaching changes, the awkward troika of Manqoba Mnqithi, Rhulani Mokwena, and Steve Komphela. But slowly, the machine was rebuilt.

Under the eventual sole leadership of Rhulani Mokwena, Sundowns returned to what had made them great. The collective was restored. Goals came from everywhere. The team averaged over two goals per game again. Multiple players reached double figures in scoring. The DStv Diski Challenge produced talent that fed into the first team. The recruitment was strategic, not star-driven.

The results followed. In 2022, Sundowns won the inaugural African Football League, a statement that they were back among the continent's elite. In 2023 and 2024, they reached back-to-back CAF Champions League semifinals. In 2025, they went further, reaching the final. They lost to Pyramids. The trophy that had eluded them since 2016 remained just out of reach.

But the hope had returned.

The contrast could not be starker. Under the Vila project, Sundowns were predictable, slow, and toothless in big moments. They crashed out in group stages or early knockout rounds. Without the project, they became a team that consistently reached the business end of the competition. Semifinals. A final. An AFL trophy. The machine was back to competing at the highest level.

The Vila years set Sundowns back. They lost three years of potential continental dominance while Pitso chased a ghost. But the foundation was strong enough to survive. And now, with the project long abandoned, Sundowns are once again knocking on the door of African glory.

It is not a matter of if, but when.

---

## The Hope

For Sundowns fans, the years between 2017 and 2020 were a frustration. A CAF Champions League-winning machine had been dismantled not by rivals, but by a coach's ego and a project that never fit. The draws, the slow starts, the goals that came only after Vila was substituted, the careers wasted, the continental campaigns that fizzled out in the group stage. It was a period of what could have been.

But the club survived. The structure held. And when the project was finally abandoned, the machine was rebuilt.

Today, Sundowns are again among the teams to beat in Africa. The squad is deep. The attack is fluid. The goals are shared. The lessons of the Vila era have been learned: systems win, not individuals. No player is bigger than the collective.

Sibusiso Vilakazi kissed a medal in Cairo without touching the ball. He scored a tap-in against Barcelona. He was called intelligent. He was good at Wits.

None of it made Sundowns better.

But Sundowns, finally, are better without him. And that trophy is coming.
