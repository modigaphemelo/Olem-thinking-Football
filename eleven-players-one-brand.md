# Eleven Players, One Brand: The Decade Kaizer Chiefs Forgot How to Build

In 2015, Kaizer Chiefs lifted the league title. It was their fourth in seven years. Stuart Baxter had squeezed every last drop from an aging squad: Tefu Mashamaite, Morgan Gould, Reneilwe Letsholonyane, Siphiwe Tshabalala, Bernard Parker, Knowledge Musona, Itumeleng Khune in his prime. They were battle-hardened, organized, and relentless.

It was the last time Kaizer Chiefs looked like a football club.

What followed was not a rebuilding phase. It was a decade of denial. Coaches came and went. Stars were signed and discarded. The academy produced talent that never played. The trophy cabinet stayed empty. And through it all, the brand grew. The stadiums stayed full. The merchandise kept selling. The media never stopped treating Chiefs as the biggest story in the country.

But a brand does not win trophies. A football club does. And for nearly a decade, Kaizer Chiefs had been the former while pretending to be the latter.

Then came the 2024 Nedbank Cup. A penalty shootout. A trophy. The drought was over. But did anything really change?

---

## The Coaching Carousel

Since Stuart Baxter left in 2015, Chiefs have employed a parade of coaches that reads like a study in institutional panic.

Steve Komphela arrived promising possession football and youth development. He lasted nearly three seasons, the longest tenure of any post-Baxter coach. He also won nothing. The "Komphela must go" campaign grew louder with each trophy-less season. In April 2018, after a Nedbank Cup semifinal loss, Komphela resigned on the spot, walking away from the job and the contract he still had left.

That moment broke something. Komphela was not the answer, but he was the closest thing to a long-term project Chiefs had. When he left, the board abandoned any pretense of patience.

Since then:

- Giovanni Solinas lasted four months
- Ernst Middendorp had two separate stints, each ending in collapse
- Gavin Hunt was fired after one season
- Arthur Zwane, a club legend, was given eighteen months before being pushed aside
- Molefi Ntseki, who had no business coaching a club of Chiefs' size, lasted even less

Each coach brought a different system. Each was given less than two years. Each was fired when the underlying problems remained. The board kept treating the coach as the problem, and the coach kept being replaced, and the problems kept getting worse.

---

## The Star Signing Fallacy

In 2018, Chiefs signed Khama Billiat from Sundowns. He was the PSL Player of the Year, a CAF Champions League winner, one of the most feared attackers in the country. The signing was meant to shift the balance of power.

Billiat arrived with the weight of a nation on his shoulders. He was supposed to be the difference-maker, the player who would end the drought. For moments, he was brilliant. He scored spectacular goals, produced magical assists, dragged Chiefs through games they had no right to win.

But the squad around him was not built to support him. He was surrounded by aging veterans, young players not yet ready, and a revolving door of coaches with different systems. By the time his contract ended, Billiat had been ground down. He left having won nothing at Chiefs. His immense talent was spent on a team that never gave him the platform to succeed.

In 2021, Keagan Dolly returned from Europe. Alongside Billiat, Chiefs finally had a partnership that could rival any in the league. For a brief period, it worked. The 2021-22 season started with Dolly and Billiat combining, and Chiefs looked like contenders.

Again, the supporting cast was not there. The midfield lacked steel. The defense was unreliable. The depth was non-existent. When injuries hit, when fatigue set in, there was no one to step up. Dolly and Billiat together should have been enough on paper. In reality, football is played with eleven players, and Chiefs had not built a team. They had collected stars.

In 2024, Gaston Sirino arrived from Sundowns. Same profile. Same expectations. Same story unfolding in real time. He produces moments. He wins matches. But look around him. The squad is still unbalanced. There is no consistent goal scorer alongside him. The team cannot decide if it wants to build around him or treat him as one piece of a puzzle that has not yet been assembled.

The pattern is clear. Chiefs sign a star from Sundowns. The star arrives with massive expectations. The star produces moments of brilliance. The star wins nothing. Then the star leaves, and the cycle repeats.

The problem is not the stars. The problem is that Chiefs sign stars and expect them to fix a team that has no identity, no structure, no foundation. Billiat and Dolly together should have won something. They did not because they were surrounded by a squad that was not built to support them.

When Sundowns sign a star, they are adding to a functioning machine. When Chiefs sign a star, they are handing the keys to a house that is falling apart and hoping the star will rebuild it alone.

---

## Develop or Buy? The Answer Is Neither

Kaizer Chiefs cannot decide what they are.

Some years they lean on free agents and out-of-contract players to avoid spending transfer fees. This brings in veterans on inflated salaries with no resale value. Other years they talk about youth development, yet the academy graduates rarely get meaningful minutes. When they do spend, it is often on the wrong players or too late.

The academy produces talent. The DStv Diski Challenge team has had moments of success. But where is the pathway? Young players train with the first team, make a handful of appearances, then disappear into the loan system or out of the club entirely. Meanwhile, Sundowns have built a production line that feeds their first team consistently, and when those players are ready, they compete for trophies.

Chiefs are stuck in the middle. Not brave enough to fully commit to youth. Not ruthless enough to outspend Sundowns in the transfer market. Not strategic enough to do either with consistency. The result is a squad that is neither young and hungry nor experienced and reliable. It is a collection of players assembled without a plan.

---

## The Motaung Problem

There is a reason the same patterns repeat regardless of the coach, regardless of the players, regardless of the season.

Kaizer Chiefs is a family business.

Kaizer Motaung founded the club. His son, Kaizer Motaung Junior, now runs football operations. The family controls everything, and they are not going anywhere. The problem is not that they do not care. The problem is that there is no accountability.

In a properly structured club, if the football operation fails for a decade, the people running it are replaced. At Chiefs, that cannot happen. The same people have been making decisions for years, with the same results, and there is no mechanism for change.

To be fair, Junior has done better than his father. The Bobby years were chaos. Agents ran the club. Signings were made for reasons other than football. The constant crisis mode was exhausting. Junior has brought stability. The Nedbank Cup trophy in 2024 was real. The recruitment has been more sensible. The constant leaks and internal politics have quietened.

But better than chaos is not the same as competing with Sundowns. The ceiling is still there, and the ceiling is set by the family structure. You cannot fire yourself. You cannot bring in a genuine sporting director with real authority if that person would outrank the family. The club will only go as far as the Motaungs can take it, and a decade of evidence suggests that is not far enough.

---

## The Sundowns Blueprint

The most painful part for Chiefs fans is that the blueprint exists. It is sitting in Chloorkop, winning league titles and competing in Africa.

Sundowns invested in infrastructure, not just players. They built a high-performance center that rivals European clubs. They hired football people to run football operations, not family members. They built a squad with depth and balance, buying the best players from rivals while also producing their own talent. They created a culture where winning is expected, not hoped for.

Chiefs had the resources to do all of this. The brand was bigger. The fan base was larger. The history was richer. They chose not to. And now they are a decade behind, still signing stars, still firing coaches, still wondering why nothing changes.

The gap between the two clubs is not on the scoreboard. It is in every department of the two institutions. One modernized. One did not.

---

## What Would It Take?

A real rebuild would require Chiefs to do things they have shown no willingness to do.

- Hire a sporting director with real authority, not a family member
- Commit to a playing identity and give a coach time to implement it, regardless of short-term results
- Build a balanced squad instead of collecting stars
- Create a genuine pathway from the academy to the first team
- Shift the culture from entitlement to hard work

Is any of this likely under the current structure? The answer is no. And that is the point.

The Nedbank Cup was a moment. It broke the drought. It stopped the narrative. But moments do not fix structural decay. Unless Chiefs finally accept that the problem is not the coach, not the players, not luck, but the way the club is run, the drought will return. The same cycles will repeat. The same excuses will be made.

---

## The Brand vs. The Club

Kaizer Chiefs is the biggest brand in South African football. The stadiums are full. The merchandise sells. The media covers every move. The name alone carries weight.

But a brand does not win trophies. A football club does.

For a decade, Chiefs have prioritized the brand over the club. They have sold nostalgia, signed big names, hired famous coaches, and done everything except build a functioning football operation. They have acted like the brand alone guarantees success, and they have been proven wrong year after year.

The 2015 title was won by a team. The players who lifted that trophy were aging, but they were organized, they were committed, and they were built to win. Since then, Chiefs have been assembling brands, not teams. They have been collecting names, not building systems. They have been hoping for magic instead of doing the work.

Eleven players take the field. They wear the same shirt. They carry the same history. But if they are not built into a team, if the institution behind them is not structured for success, then all that remains is the brand. And the brand alone has never won anything.

---

## The Final Question

The Nedbank Cup bought Chiefs time. It gave the fans something to celebrate. It let the club pretend that the corner had been turned.

But the structural problems remain. The family still runs the club without accountability. The recruitment still lacks a coherent philosophy. The coaching carousel has only slowed, not stopped. The academy still produces talent that does not play.

The question is whether Chiefs will use the oxygen from that trophy to finally do the hard work of rebuilding, or whether they will treat it as proof that nothing needs to change.

A decade of evidence suggests the answer is already clear.

The brand will survive. The merchandise will sell. The stadiums will stay full.

But the trophies will stay in the past.
