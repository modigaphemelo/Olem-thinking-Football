# Nietzsche Was Right: Sometimes You Need Stupidity

The courage to finish what you start.

There's a moment after every new beginning where the excitement fades and the real work begins. The idea is launched. The project is started. You're committed. And somewhere between the thrill of "yes" and the satisfaction of "done," something happens.

You get distracted.

A new idea pops up. A different approach catches your eye. Someone shares something "better" you should try. You tell yourself you're staying flexible. You're exploring options. But the options multiply, and soon you can't remember which path you were on. You end up with a dozen things started and nothing finished.

Nietzsche saw this clearly. In The Gay Science, he wrote:

> “A sign of strong character, when once the resolution has been taken, to shut the ear even to the best counter-arguments. Occasionally, therefore, a will to stupidity.”

He didn't mean ignore good advice. He meant that once you've decided, constantly reopening the decision is not wisdom. It's weakness. The person who finishes things knows when to stop listening and keep walking.

## The Problem Isn’t Starting

For many of us, starting is easy. Too easy. A spark of inspiration, a burst of energy, and we're off. New project. New habit. New skill. Feels great.

The problem comes later. When the work gets boring. When it gets hard. When something shinier appears.

- That’s when we tell ourselves stories:
    "This approach isn’t working, I should try that other one."
- "I’ve learned enough from this, time for something fresh."
- "Actually, that new thing is more important right now."

These stories feel reasonable. They’re often just escape routes. We’re not quitting — we’re "pivoting." We’re not distracted — we’re "exploring." But the result is the same: another thing left at 90%, another finish line we never cross.

## What Never Finishing Costs You

We're good at calculating the risks of sticking with something. We rarely calculate the risks of always jumping.

Think about what chronic non-finishing actually costs:

- **Expertise**: You can’t get good at something by doing the first 20% over and over.
- **Trust**: People notice when you’re a starter but not a finisher.
- **Self-respect**: Every abandoned commitment quietly tells you your word doesn’t matter.
- **Fulfillment**: There’s a specific satisfaction in *done* that nothing else provides.

These costs add up silently. One day you look back and realize you've done a lot of beginning and very little arriving.

## How to Know When to Ignore the New

There's no perfect formula, but these questions help:

1.  Are you past the halfway point? If yes, the fastest path to done is forward.
2.  Is the new thing genuinely better, or just different and easier?
3.  Have you noticed this pattern before — getting restless right when things get hard?
4.  What would it feel like to actually finish this, just once?

If your answers point toward staying, the smartest thing you can do is be stubborn. Shut your ears. Keep going.

## Practical Ways to Stay on Track

1.  **Name the pattern**
    Call it what it is. "I’m quitting because I’m bored." "I’m chasing shiny again." Naming it removes the disguise.

2.  **Set a "no new" rule**
    Until X is done, no new projects in that category. Write it down. Tell someone.

3.  **Make finishing visible**
    Put a physical reminder where you’ll see it daily. A sticky note. A progress bar. Something that says "finish this first."

4.  **Ask what "done" looks like**
    Get specific. What exactly counts as finished? Vague goals are easier to abandon.

5.  **Remember the feeling**
    Recall a time you actually finished something hard. That feeling. You want more of that.

## What Changes When You Finish

Here's what I've learned: the excitement of starting always fades. You will never stay permanently inspired. The work will always, at some point, become *work*.

But when you finish anyway, something shifts. You build something real. You prove something to yourself. The scattered person becomes the reliable one. The perpetual beginner becomes someone who completes.

Nietzsche called this strength of character. Not brilliance. Not endless openness. The simple ability to decide and then shut your ears long enough to arrive.

Sometimes you need the will to be stubborn. To finish what's boring. To complete what you started, even when something else calls. The finishing itself will teach you what a thousand new beginnings never could.
